The transit method, in plain words

Kepler stared at 150,000 stars and watched for tiny, repeating dips in brightness. A dip can mean a planet — or an impostor. Here's how scientists tell them apart.

🌑

A planet makes a dip

When a planet crosses in front of its star, it blocks a sliver of light. The star dims by a few hundred parts per million, then brightens again — once per orbit.

👯

Impostors make big dips

Two stars orbiting each other (an eclipsing binary) block far more light — a "planet" tens of Earth-radii wide is really a second star. Depth and inferred radius give it away.

📉

Noise makes weak dips

Low signal-to-noise or a grazing, off-center crossing often turns out to be a false positive. The model weighs all of this at once.
